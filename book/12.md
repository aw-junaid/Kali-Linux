## Chapter 12 – Bluetooth & RFID

Bluetooth and RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) are two of the most pervasive wireless technologies in modern environments, powering everything from wearables, smart locks, and medical devices to access control badges, contactless payment cards, and inventory tracking tags. Because both technologies broadcast data over the air, often with weak or legacy cryptographic protections, they represent a rich and frequently under-tested attack surface during physical security assessments, red team engagements, and IoT penetration tests.

This chapter covers the discovery, enumeration, and exploitation of Bluetooth Classic, Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), and RFID/NFC systems using tools available natively or installable on Kali Linux. Bluetooth reconnaissance allows an assessor to fingerprint nearby devices, identify vulnerable BLE GATT services, and intercept or inject traffic. RFID and MIFARE Classic attacks allow an assessor to clone access cards, recover encryption keys through cryptographic weaknesses (such as the Crypto-1 cipher used in MIFARE Classic), and emulate arbitrary tags for physical access bypass testing.

The tools covered in this chapter span passive monitoring, active scanning, protocol-level interaction, and full hardware emulation:

- **BlueHydra** – A Bluetooth device discovery and tracking daemon that aggregates Classic and BLE device data over time, ideal for long-duration passive Bluetooth reconnaissance.
- **Bettercap BLE** – The BLE module of the versatile Bettercap framework, used for active BLE scanning, GATT enumeration, characteristic read/write, and BLE MITM-style interaction.
- **ChameleonMini** – An open-source hardware platform for RFID/NFC tag emulation, cloning, and sniffing, widely used to impersonate access control cards and study RFID protocols at the physical and logical layer.
- **MFCUK** (MiFare Classic Universal toolKit) – A tool implementing the "darkside attack" to recover MIFARE Classic keys when a card is vulnerable to nonce-based cryptographic weaknesses, particularly useful when no known keys exist yet.
- **MFOC** (MiFare Classic Offline Cracker) – A tool implementing the "nested attack" to recover all sector keys on a MIFARE Classic card once at least one key is already known, commonly used after MFCUK or with a default/known key.


#### Legal and Ethical Notice

All techniques, tools, and commands documented in this chapter are intended strictly for authorized security testing, research, and educational purposes. Bluetooth interception, BLE characteristic manipulation, and RFID card cloning against systems or devices you do not own or lack explicit written authorization to test may violate computer misuse, wiretapping, and access control laws in your jurisdiction. Always operate within the scope of a signed engagement or on hardware you personally own in a lab environment.

### 12.1 BlueHydra

#### 1. Tool Introduction

BlueHydra is a Bluetooth device discovery service written in Ruby, originally developed by Bluetooth researchers associated with the PwnPlug/PwnieExpress family of tools and now maintained by the community, including a Kali-focused fork maintained by ZerBea. Unlike a simple `hcitool scan`, BlueHydra runs as a persistent background daemon that continuously monitors both Bluetooth Classic (BR/EDR) and Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) advertisements, aggregating everything it sees into a local SQLite database.

BlueHydra's core value is **temporal tracking**. It doesn't just tell you "device X is here now" — it tracks first-seen and last-seen timestamps, RSSI (signal strength) history, vendor/manufacturer identification via OUI lookups, device class, and service UUIDs for every Bluetooth device that passes within range over the lifetime of the capture session. This makes it exceptionally useful for:

- Long-duration passive reconnaissance during a physical penetration test or red team engagement
- Identifying badge/fob-style BLE beacons used in access control or asset tracking
- Building a historical map of Bluetooth device presence in a target facility
- Detecting rogue or unexpected Bluetooth devices (skimmers, unauthorized peripherals, planted trackers)

BlueHydra uses the system's Bluetooth stack (BlueZ) under the hood, driving `hcitool`, `hcidump`, and `bluetoothctl`-equivalent functionality via Ruby wrappers, and can work with a standard internal/USB Bluetooth adapter as well as external adapters like the Ubertooth One for lower-level packet capture.

#### 7. Common Use Cases

- **Physical penetration test reconnaissance**: Leave BlueHydra running for the duration of an on-site engagement to build a complete inventory of Bluetooth devices present, including employee wearables, headsets, and potential rogue devices.
- **Asset and badge discovery**: Many modern access-control badges and asset trackers use BLE beaconing; BlueHydra will passively catalog these without alerting anyone.
- **Rogue device / skimmer detection**: Because BlueHydra tracks first-seen and RSSI, an unusually persistent or newly appeared BLE device near a point-of-sale terminal or ATM can indicate a planted skimmer.
- **Red team pattern-of-life analysis**: Tracking which known devices appear at what times of day can support building a target's schedule/pattern of life.
- **Long-term facility monitoring**: Combined with a Raspberry Pi and external battery, BlueHydra can run unattended for days as a covert or authorized monitoring implant during an engagement.

#### 8. Automation with Bash

The following script automates starting BlueHydra, letting it run for a fixed duration, then exporting results and cleanly stopping it — useful for timeboxed assessments.

```bash
#!/bin/bash
# bluehydra_auto.sh - Automated timed BlueHydra capture and export
# Usage: sudo ./bluehydra_auto.sh <duration_minutes> <output_csv>

DURATION_MIN="${1:-30}"
OUTPUT_CSV="${2:-bluehydra_report_$(date +%Y%m%d_%H%M).csv}"
BH_DIR="/opt/blue_hydra"
DB_PATH="${BH_DIR}/blue_hydra.db"

if [[ $EUID -ne 0 ]]; then
  echo "[-] This script must be run as root."
  exit 1
fi

echo "[*] Starting BlueHydra daemon for ${DURATION_MIN} minute(s)..."
cd "$BH_DIR" || exit 1
ruby bin/blue_hydra -d -k

sleep 3
PID=$(cat "${BH_DIR}/blue_hydra.pid" 2>/dev/null)
if [[ -z "$PID" ]]; then
  echo "[-] Failed to start BlueHydra."
  exit 1
fi
echo "[+] BlueHydra running with PID ${PID}"

# Wait for the specified duration
sleep "$((DURATION_MIN * 60))"

echo "[*] Duration elapsed. Stopping BlueHydra..."
kill -SIGINT "$PID"
sleep 5

echo "[*] Exporting results to ${OUTPUT_CSV}..."
sqlite3 -header -csv "$DB_PATH" \
  "SELECT address, name, vendor, le, classic_mode, last_seen FROM blue_hydra_devices;" > "$OUTPUT_CSV"

DEVICE_COUNT=$(($(wc -l < "$OUTPUT_CSV") - 1))
echo "[+] Capture complete. ${DEVICE_COUNT} devices logged to ${OUTPUT_CSV}"
```

Example run:

```bash
sudo ./bluehydra_auto.sh 15 site_survey_lobby.csv
```

Output:

```
[*] Starting BlueHydra daemon for 15 minute(s)...
[+] BlueHydra running with PID 20933
[*] Duration elapsed. Stopping BlueHydra...
[*] Exporting results to site_survey_lobby.csv...
[+] Capture complete. 38 devices logged to site_survey_lobby.csv
```

#### 9. Tips and Best Practices

- Always run BlueHydra with `-k` (keep-alive) during long engagements; without it, BlueHydra can exit prematurely if no devices are momentarily visible.
- Pair BlueHydra with an Ubertooth One when possible — it significantly improves BLE advertisement capture reliability versus a stock internal Bluetooth adapter.
- Periodically back up `blue_hydra.db` during multi-day engagements; it is a plain SQLite file and easy to copy with `cp` while the daemon is stopped or paused.
- Use `rfkill list` and `rfkill unblock bluetooth` before starting BlueHydra if the adapter appears blocked or unresponsive.
- Cross-reference discovered MAC address vendor prefixes (OUIs) against known access-control and asset-tracking manufacturers to prioritize which devices are worth investigating further with Bettercap BLE.
- Run BlueHydra from a dedicated non-critical adapter if you're simultaneously using another adapter for active BLE interaction with Bettercap, to avoid HCI resource contention.

#### 10. Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| `blue_hydra: command not found` | Not installed via symlink, or bundler gems missing | Run from source with `ruby bin/blue_hydra`, or re-run `bundle install` |
| No devices ever appear | Bluetooth adapter blocked or missing permissions | Run `rfkill unblock bluetooth` and confirm with `hciconfig -a` that `hci0` is `UP RUNNING` |
| `Errno::EPERM` on startup | Not run as root | Prefix all commands with `sudo` |
| BlueHydra exits immediately with no devices | Keep-alive not set | Add `-k` flag |
| Database locked errors when querying with `sqlite3` | BlueHydra daemon holding a write lock | Stop the daemon before querying, or use `sqlite3 blue_hydra.db ".timeout 5000"` |
| Gem install failures (`libsqlite3-dev` missing) | Missing system headers | `sudo apt install libsqlite3-dev libbluetooth-dev` then re-run `bundle install` |
| Duplicate/ghost entries for the same physical device | BLE MAC address randomization (privacy feature on iOS/Android) | Correlate by manufacturer data / advertised service UUIDs rather than MAC address alone |
| High CPU usage over multi-day runs | Large SQLite DB with no pruning | Periodically archive and truncate `blue_hydra.db`, or set a shorter `rssi_log_interval` |

### 12.2 Bettercap BLE

#### 1. Tool Introduction

Bettercap is a modular, extensible network attack and monitoring framework written in Go, widely known for its Wi-Fi, ARP spoofing, and man-in-the-middle capabilities. Less well known, but equally powerful, is its **`ble` module**, which turns Bettercap into a full-featured Bluetooth Low Energy reconnaissance and interaction tool.

The BLE module allows an assessor to:

- Actively scan for nearby BLE peripherals and advertisements
- Enumerate GATT (Generic Attribute Profile) services and characteristics exposed by a BLE device
- Read and write arbitrary characteristic values, including those not intended to be user-accessible
- Interact with BLE devices without pairing, when the device does not enforce authentication on specific characteristics
- Script BLE interaction using Bettercap's caplet (`.cap`) automation files

BLE is used extensively in smart locks, fitness trackers, medical devices, industrial sensors, and increasingly in physical access control systems that use a phone or fob as a credential. Because many BLE implementations expose readable/writable characteristics without proper authentication, Bettercap's `ble` module is often the fastest way to discover an insecure implementation — for example, a smart lock that accepts an "unlock" command written directly to a characteristic with no PIN or encryption check.

Bettercap's BLE support uses the host system's Bluetooth adapter via BlueZ (on Linux) and requires a BLE-capable adapter (virtually all modern internal and USB Bluetooth 4.0+ adapters support this).

```bash
#!/bin/bash
# ble_audit.sh - Automated BLE recon + enumeration sweep using Bettercap
# Usage: sudo ./ble_audit.sh <interface> <recon_seconds> <output_dir>

IFACE="${1:-hci0}"
RECON_TIME="${2:-30}"
OUTDIR="${3:-ble_audit_$(date +%Y%m%d_%H%M)}"

  "ble.recon on; sleep ${RECON_TIME}; ble.show json > ${DEVICE_JSON}; ble.recon off; exit" \
  -no-colors > "${OUTDIR}/recon.log" 2>&1

if [[ ! -s "$DEVICE_JSON" ]]; then
  echo "[-] No devices discovered or export failed. Check ${OUTDIR}/recon.log"

echo "[*] Enumerating GATT services for each discovered device..."
> "$ENUM_LOG"

  bettercap -iface "$IFACE" -eval "ble.enum ${MAC}; exit" -no-colors >> "$ENUM_LOG" 2>&1
  echo "" >> "$ENUM_LOG"
done

echo "[+] Enumeration complete. Results in ${ENUM_LOG}"
```

#### 9. Tips and Best Practices

- Always run `ble.recon.timeout` for scripted/unattended sessions to avoid Bettercap hanging indefinitely.
- Devices that require pairing will refuse `ble.enum` connections; try `bluetoothctl pair <MAC>` first if authorized to do so, then retry.
- Write operations (`ble.write`) can trigger real physical actions (unlocking doors, triggering alarms) — only test against authorized lab equipment or within a signed engagement scope.
- Use `events.stream on` during long recon windows to catch devices that briefly appear and disappear (common with phones due to MAC randomization).
- Convert ASCII commands to hex before using `ble.write` — e.g., `echo -n "UNLOCK" | xxd -p` to generate the hex payload.
- Run only one Bluetooth-heavy tool (Bettercap, BlueHydra, hcitool) at a time per adapter; simultaneous HCI access from multiple tools can cause connection instability.

#### 10. Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| `ble.recon` reports "no BLE-capable adapter found" | Adapter doesn't support BLE or is blocked | Check with `hciconfig -a`; run `rfkill unblock bluetooth` |
| `ble.enum` hangs indefinitely | Device out of range or connection interval mismatch | Set `set ble.connect.timeout 10` to force a timeout |
| `ble.write` returns "write not permitted" | Characteristic requires authentication/encryption | Confirm characteristic properties with `ble.enum`; some writes are intentionally protected |
| Devices show as `(unknown)` with no name | Device did not broadcast a name in its advertisement packet | Use `ble.enum` to read the GAP Device Name characteristic directly (UUID `00002a00-...`) |
| Bettercap crashes on `ble.recon on` | BlueZ service not running or conflicting with NetworkManager | `sudo systemctl restart bluetooth` before relaunching Bettercap |
| Permission denied errors | Not run with `sudo` | Bettercap requires raw HCI socket access; always run as root |
| Duplicate MACs appearing as different devices | BLE MAC randomization by the peripheral OS | Correlate using GATT service UUIDs or manufacturer data instead of MAC alone |

### 12.3 ChameleonMini

#### 1. Tool Introduction

ChameleonMini is an open-source hardware and firmware platform designed for RFID/NFC security research, emulation, and cloning at the 13.56 MHz frequency range (ISO14443A, MIFARE Classic, MIFARE Ultralight, and related protocols). Unlike software-only tools that interact with a card reader, ChameleonMini is a small standalone hardware device (based on an ATxmega microcontroller) that can:

- **Emulate** an RFID/NFC tag, presenting itself to a reader as if it were a genuine access card
- **Sniff** communication between a real card and a real reader in a man-in-the-middle configuration
- **Clone** a card's UID and full memory dump onto the device for later replay
- **Store multiple card profiles/slots** in onboard memory (typically 8 slots), switchable via button press or serial command
- **Log** raw communication traces for offline protocol analysis

Because ChameleonMini operates at the RF physical layer and firmware level, it is one of the most capable tools for physical access control assessments — testing whether a facility's badge readers can be bypassed via a cloned or emulated card. It is controlled via a serial USB CDC interface, meaning it can be driven directly from Kali Linux using a terminal program (`screen`, `minicom`) or a dedicated Python client library.

ChameleonMini is not a passive analysis tool alone — it is an active emulation device, meaning it can genuinely fool a badge reader into granting access if loaded with a valid cloned card profile, making it a critical tool for red team physical access engagements.

### 12.4 MFCUK

#### 1. Tool Introduction

MFCUK (MiFare Classic Universal toolKit) is a command-line utility used to recover encryption keys from MIFARE Classic RFID cards when **no keys are known in advance**. It implements the **"darkside attack"**, a cryptographic weakness discovered in the MIFARE Classic Crypto-1 stream cipher that exploits flawed random nonce generation during the authentication handshake.

MIFARE Classic cards divide their memory into sectors, each protected by two 48-bit keys (Key A and Key B). Before MFOC's faster "nested attack" (covered in section 12.5) can be used, at least one valid key for the card must already be known. MFCUK exists specifically to solve the **bootstrap problem**: recovering that first key from a completely unknown card using only a compatible NFC reader, with zero prior knowledge.

The darkside attack works by exploiting the fact that the pseudo-random number generator (PRNG) used in the card's authentication process is not cryptographically secure and, critically, the PRNG state is not properly reset between authentication attempts on many MIFARE Classic implementations. By sending crafted authentication requests and analyzing the card's encrypted NACK (negative acknowledgment) responses, MFCUK can statistically recover key bits over a large number of attempts.

Because this attack works purely against the card's flawed cryptographic implementation (not the reader), MFCUK requires only a compatible PC/SC or libnfc-supported reader (such as a PN532-based device or ACR122U) and physical access to the target card — no access to the legitimate reader infrastructure is required.

#### 4. All Command-Line Options

| Flag | Description |
|------|-------------|

#### 8. Automation with Bash

```bash
#!/bin/bash
# mfcuk_sweep.sh - Automated darkside attack sweep across all sectors of a MIFARE Classic 1K card
# Usage: sudo ./mfcuk_sweep.sh <output_dir>

OUTDIR="${1:-mfcuk_results_$(date +%Y%m%d_%H%M)}"
mkdir -p "$OUTDIR"
STEPS=1000
SETS=500

echo "[*] Confirming card presence..."
if ! mfcuk -c | grep -q "Card detected"; then
  echo "[-] No card detected. Place a card on the reader and retry."
  exit 1
fi

echo "[+] Card detected. Beginning full sector sweep (0-15, Key A and B)..."

for SECTOR in $(seq 0 15); do
  for KEYTYPE in A B; do
    TARGET="${KEYTYPE}${SECTOR}"
    OUTFILE="${OUTDIR}/sector${SECTOR}_key${KEYTYPE}.mfd"
    echo "[*] Attacking sector ${SECTOR}, Key ${KEYTYPE}..."
    RESULT=$(mfcuk -C -R "$TARGET" -s "$SETS" -S "$STEPS" -O "$OUTFILE" 2>&1)

    if echo "$RESULT" | grep -q "Recovered Key"; then
      KEY=$(echo "$RESULT" | grep "Recovered Key" | awk '{print $NF}')
      echo "[+] Sector ${SECTOR} Key ${KEYTYPE}: ${KEY}"
      echo "${SECTOR},${KEYTYPE},${KEY}" >> "${OUTDIR}/recovered_keys.csv"
    else
      echo "[-] Sector ${SECTOR} Key ${KEYTYPE}: FAILED"
      echo "${SECTOR},${KEYTYPE},FAILED" >> "${OUTDIR}/recovered_keys.csv"
    fi
  done
done

echo "[+] Sweep complete. Results summary:"
cat "${OUTDIR}/recovered_keys.csv"
```

#### 9. Tips and Best Practices

- MFCUK's darkside attack can take anywhere from seconds to many minutes per key depending on the specific card's PRNG behavior — be patient and increase `-S` steps if initial attempts fail.
- Once you recover even a single valid key with MFCUK, switch to MFOC (section 12.5) for the remaining sectors — the nested attack is dramatically faster.
- Keep the card completely still and close to the reader antenna throughout the attack; RF instability causes failed authentication attempts and wasted cycles.
- Not all MIFARE Classic cards are vulnerable — some deployments use modified/hardened PRNG implementations (sometimes marketed as "MIFARE Classic EV1" hardened variants) that resist the darkside attack entirely.
- Save every `.mfd` output file with a clear naming convention (sector + key type) so partial results can be merged later into a complete card dump.
- Use `nfc-list` before every attack session to confirm consistent reader/card detection, as intermittent RF issues are a common cause of failed attempts that look like cryptographic failures.

#### 10. Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| `nfc-list` shows no device | Reader not connected or driver conflict with PC/SC daemon | Run `sudo systemctl stop pcscd` if libnfc conflicts with the smart card daemon, then retry |
| Attack runs but never succeeds after thousands of attempts | Card uses hardened/non-vulnerable PRNG | Try MFOC first with common default keys instead; darkside attack may not apply |
| `mfcuk: command not found` | Package not installed | `sudo apt install mfcuk` |
| Frequent "tag lost" or communication errors mid-attack | Card moved or too far from antenna | Keep card fixed in position; use tape or a card holder jig for stability during long attacks |
| Recovered key doesn't authenticate correctly with MFOC afterward | Copy/transcription error of the hex key | Always use the `.mfd` output file directly instead of manually retyping recovered keys |
| Attack extremely slow (many minutes per sector) | Low `-S` step count relative to card's noise characteristics | Increase `-S` value (e.g., from 1000 to 3000) for more reliable statistical convergence |
| `libnfc` reports "unable to claim USB interface" | Conflicting process holding the reader | Check `lsof /dev/bus/usb/...` or reboot to release the device handle |

### 12.5 MFOC

#### 1. Tool Introduction

MFOC (MiFare Classic Offline Cracker) is a command-line tool for recovering **all** sector keys on a MIFARE Classic card once **at least one valid key is already known** — whether that key came from a common default key list, a previous MFCUK darkside attack (section 12.4), or prior knowledge of the target system. MFOC implements the **"nested attack"**, a cryptographic technique that leverages one authenticated session to efficiently recover the keys of other sectors on the same card, exploiting weaknesses in the Crypto-1 cipher's nonce generation once a trusted authentication baseline exists.

Where MFCUK is slow and used to bootstrap a single key from zero knowledge, MFOC is dramatically faster (often recovering an entire card's key set in under a minute) because it doesn't need to break the cipher from scratch for every sector — it reuses timing and nonce relationships from the already-authenticated sector to statistically derive the others.

MFOC also includes a built-in dictionary of common/default MIFARE Classic keys (`FFFFFFFFFFFF`, `A0A1A2A3A4A5`, `000000000000`, and dozens of known transit/access-control system defaults), meaning it will often successfully attack a card with zero prior key knowledge if the target uses factory-default or well-known keys — no MFCUK step required in those cases.

Upon successful key recovery, MFOC can also produce a complete `.mfd` binary dump of the card's full memory contents, ready for analysis, modification, or re-writing to a blank/magic card via tools like `mfoc`'s companion `nfc-mfsetuid` or a ChameleonMini upload.

#### 4. All Command-Line Options

| Flag | Description |
|------|-------------|
| `-h` | Displays the help/usage menu |
| `-k <hex_key>` | Supplies a known 12-hex-digit (6-byte) key to seed the nested attack |
| `-f <keyfile>` | Loads a newline-separated dictionary file of candidate keys to try before attempting the nested attack |
| `-O <file>` | Writes the final recovered full-card dump to the specified `.mfd` file |
| `-D <file>` | Loads a pre-existing `.mfd` dump as a baseline (useful when merging with MFCUK-recovered partial data) |
| `-P <probes>` | Sets the number of authentication probes used per nested attack attempt (affects speed/reliability tradeoff) |
| `-T <tolerance>` | Sets the timing tolerance value for nested attack nonce distance calculations |
| `-s <sector>` | Restricts the attack to a specific sector number instead of the full card |
| `-v` | Enables verbose output for progress and statistical monitoring |
| `-R` | Forces a retry of previously failed sectors instead of skipping them |
| `-a` | Uses only Key A during the attack process, skipping Key B attempts |
| `-b` | Uses only Key B during the attack process, skipping Key A attempts |

#### 7. Common Use Cases

- **Completing a card key set after MFCUK bootstrap**: The standard workflow is MFCUK to recover one key, then MFOC with `-k` to rapidly recover the rest.
- **Fast auditing of factory-default deployments**: Many organizations never rotate MIFARE Classic keys from their factory defaults; MFOC's built-in dictionary alone often fully compromises such cards in under a minute with zero prior knowledge.
- **Producing a full card image for cloning**: The `.mfd` dump produced by MFOC is the standard input format for writing to "magic" MIFARE Classic cards (UID-changeable) or uploading into a ChameleonMini slot.
- **Access control system security assessment reporting**: Demonstrating full key/data recovery time provides concrete evidence for a penetration test report on the weakness of a facility's card system.
- **Card data forensic analysis**: Once decrypted, sector contents can reveal facility codes, employee ID mappings, or loyalty program balances stored in plaintext or lightly obfuscated form.

#### 8. Automation with Bash

```bash
#!/bin/bash
# mfoc_auto_recover.sh - Automated MFOC recovery with fallback to a custom keyfile
# Usage: sudo ./mfoc_auto_recover.sh <output_dir> [known_key]

OUTDIR="${1:-mfoc_results_$(date +%Y%m%d_%H%M)}"
KNOWN_KEY="$2"
KEYFILE="common_keys.txt"
mkdir -p "$OUTDIR"
DUMP_FILE="${OUTDIR}/full_dump.mfd"
LOG_FILE="${OUTDIR}/mfoc.log"

echo "[*] Confirming card presence..."
if ! nfc-list | grep -q "target(s) found"; then
  echo "[-] No card detected on reader. Aborting."
  exit 1
fi

# Build a small default keyfile if one isn't already present
if [[ ! -f "$KEYFILE" ]]; then
  cat > "$KEYFILE" << 'EOF'
FFFFFFFFFFFF
000000000000
A0A1A2A3A4A5
B0B1B2B3B4B5
D3F7D3F7D3F7
4D3A99C351DD
1A2B3C4D5E6F
EOF
  echo "[*] Created default keyfile: ${KEYFILE}"
fi

echo "[*] Attempting MFOC recovery..."
if [[ -n "$KNOWN_KEY" ]]; then
  echo "[*] Using supplied known key: ${KNOWN_KEY}"
  mfoc -k "$KNOWN_KEY" -O "$DUMP_FILE" -v > "$LOG_FILE" 2>&1
else
  echo "[*] No known key supplied, trying dictionary: ${KEYFILE}"
  mfoc -f "$KEYFILE" -O "$DUMP_FILE" -v > "$LOG_FILE" 2>&1
fi

if grep -q "Card dump written" "$LOG_FILE"; then
  echo "[+] Success. Full dump saved to ${DUMP_FILE}"
  grep "key A\|key B" "$LOG_FILE" | tee "${OUTDIR}/recovered_keys_summary.txt"
else
  echo "[-] MFOC could not recover the card automatically."
  echo "[-] Recommend running MFCUK (section 12.4) first to bootstrap a key."
  tail -10 "$LOG_FILE"
  exit 1
fi
```

#### 9. Tips and Best Practices

- Always try MFOC's built-in default dictionary first (no `-k` flag needed) before resorting to MFCUK — a large percentage of real-world MIFARE Classic deployments still use factory-default keys and will fall immediately.
- When chaining from MFCUK, pass the recovered key directly with `-k` rather than relying only on the dictionary, to guarantee the nested attack seeds correctly.
- Keep the card stationary throughout the entire nested attack — even brief RF dropouts can cause individual sector failures requiring a `-R` retry pass.
- Maintain a running site-specific keyfile (`-f`) as you encounter custom keys across an engagement; many organizations reuse the same custom key across all their cards.
- Use `-v` verbose mode when a card is behaving inconsistently, to distinguish between "no key found" and "nonce distance not converging" failure modes.
- Store all `.mfd` dumps with clear naming and checksums (`md5sum`) as part of evidence handling for a penetration test report.

#### 10. Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| "No default keys matched any sector" | Card uses non-default, unknown keys | Run MFCUK first to bootstrap one key via the darkside attack, then re-run MFOC with `-k` |
| Attack recovers some sectors but fails others | RF instability or a specific sector using an unusually different key | Use `-R` to retry only failed sectors; ensure card remains still |
| `mfoc: command not found` | Package not installed | `sudo apt install mfoc` |
| Nested attack extremely slow | Low `-P` probe count causing repeated retries | Increase `-P` value for more reliable (if slower) convergence |
| `nfc-list` doesn't detect the card during MFOC run | Reader conflict with `pcscd` service | `sudo systemctl stop pcscd` before running libnfc-based tools |
| Dump file appears incomplete or wrong size | Attack was interrupted mid-run | Re-run MFOC with `-D` pointing to the partial dump to resume/merge |
| Key recovered by MFOC doesn't match what MFCUK found earlier | Card has different keys per sector (common in properly configured systems) | This is expected — MFOC recovers per-sector keys independently once one sector is bootstrapped |
